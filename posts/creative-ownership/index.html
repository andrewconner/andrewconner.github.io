Creative Ownership

20 July 2022

Prior sources:

  • Everything is Remix (and Part 2)
  • The Rise and Fall of Cultural Appropriation
  • Ametora: How Japan Saved American Style

When you create and release art, to what extent should you retain control over its acceptance, use, and impact? How much can I own a concept or idea?

I had a recent conversation with someone who expressed concerns that Dall·E 2 and similar models will create derivate works, modeled after specific artist, cultural, and art movement styles, without appropriate social and economic recognition to the original creators.

Why is this bad? Is it bad? Why does recognition matter? To what extent is art a contribution to humanity?

Derivative creativity

There’s a natural lifecycle of creation:

  1. Innumerable influences impact artistic expression. Some of these are perceptible, some are determined by unconscious tacit cultural environments.
  2. Creatives synthesize other prior work and offer new contributions, varying content, style, and form, to express ideas. Some of these ideas are deeply personal, and some aren’t.
  3. Each work contribution serves as an explicit expression of an idea, using a specific grammar. Some artists venture further from previous conventions, creating new styles, forms, and genres.
  4. To the extent that the art resonates with others, it is appreciated and contributes to (1).

This is descriptive of how things are, and seem typical across times and cultures, regardless of specific intellectual property laws. In this exploration, we care about:

  • the ownership inside this process (dealing with specific creations, and to a lesser extent specific styles)
  • allocation of social and economic resources
  • how cultural wealth is built

Cultural wealth is the sum and accumulation of the available artistic expression ideas, styles, and forms available. Think of it as the depth of language: more words means more ideas can be expressed precisely.

Art is popular, thus contributes to cultural wealth, when it resonates with many people. This often means it’s familiar—or sufficiently familiar to understand and embrace the ideas being offered through the artistic grammar used. As such, truly fringe art is rarely immediately popular: it’s offering new ideas that are less palatable to the masses.

I’ll offer explicit goals that society should directly optimize for regarding creative works:

  1. Promote creative expression.
  2. Propagate good artistic ideas, encouraging iteration and re-use to create derivative works. Minimize rent seeking.
  3. Provide protection of how certain art is used.

Promoting creative expression

All creative work is derivative of the experiences of the creator, and when published, is an offering to society. Just as personal rights are constrained by other people’s rights, creative ownership is constrained by a culture’s collective ownership of artistic ideas. To the extent that no one is truly economically self-made, no one is creatively self-made.

Let’s look at two possible extremes:

All creative works, including impact, is owned and controlled exclusively by the creator. No derivative works or cultural contributions may be made. All references to the work must be cited.

This is obviously the least tenable. In this case, ideas would hardly ever spread, and culture could not innovate. In fact, language would be impossible, and societies could not improve upon existing ideas.

All creative work is immediately owned by the public, with no social or economic recognition of the creator.

This becomes counter-productive quickly: while it is optimized for the spread and iteration of ideas, there’s minimal incentive to actually create new ideas.

So, our ideal recognizes a tension between creative rights and the public’s ownership. From our model of creation above, each artistic expression represents a discrete piece (a dance, painting, sculpture, story, etc), as well as implicit contributions to form (genres, styles, mediums, etc). To balance artistic ownership and public ownership, it’s reasonable to consider these separately: we provide more protections to the piece than the form, to encourage iteration and further expression, while preserving social and economic incentives of the artistic work.

When I steal an idea without attribution, the core issue is that I’m socially or economically benefiting [maybe: write more about benefit?] from someone else’s ideas without doing the work. When a student plagiarizes their essay, they’re violating a social expectation that they did work. The teacher is trying to teach and encourage research and synthesis. The expectation is not that all ideas presented are new, but that the synthesis is unique. Likewise, if I copy someone else’s musical composition and release it to great cultural acclaim, I’m benefiting economically without doing the deep work of unique synthesis of musical ideas—and equally, potentially counterfactually harming someone who did the work.

We can reduce a simplistic a justice-oriented argument that creative acts are “due” recognition to a more stable model that recognizes social and economic impact of creative work.

If I have no social or economic incentive to publish my best ideas, our society misses out on potential upside. We want to encourage people spending time and effort towards creating great things, and if we rob creators from the upside, we will not realize cultural innovation in the arts and sciences that we otherwise could. We want to protect creative ownership to explicitly encourage more creative expression. If my art is always taken without attribution, I have little incentive—besides artistic self-expression and having a creative impact on others—to share my art. And practically, if I’m a good artist (as judged by culture), I should be able to exchange my art for a livelihood.

Failure modes:

  1. People with power can take good artistic ideas from people without power and make money from them, without compensating the creators.

What injustice was done? For example, if I write a silly song for fun, and someone remixes it into a viral hit—thus receiving social recognition and economic opportunity for it—am I worse off? Not directly, but there is a notable counterfactual: if I had the appropriate platform, social status, or power, I may have received the recognition instead. So in a sense, there was an injustice where I missed out on benefit. (I’m intentionally ignoring the case of me not wanting my song to spread, and it’s spread against my will. I’ll come back to this.)

To make this tangible, let’s consider three variants of cultural borrowing:

White rappers emulating the style of black rappers in America.

Black artists innovated a new style, deriving from other artistic forms (poetry, jazz, rock and roll, disco, etc) and innovating in meaningful ways. This art form was spread first in Black communities, and then increasingly widely. The wide distribution is a feature: this is an explicit cultural recognition of the value of the artistic expression. White rappers who grew up in similar cultural environments joined, emulating what they observed and iterating on the form. Then, over time, white rappers who did not grow up in similar cultural environments copy the style for (examples: Lil Dicky, a white comedian rapper, gets a TV show; Elvis copied Roy Hamilton).

As The Rise and Fall of Cultural Appropriation points out, power structures that emulate art forms from those with less social and economic power has tangible and real externalities. Widespread adoption of artistic ideas represents a cultural acceptance of the ideas — and this is the goal.

The real injustice is the extent that powerful groups benefit socially and economically without due recognition (social currency and dollar currency) to the original creators. We want to recognize the original creators to encourage more creation, as well as allocating resources “equitably”. It’s not fair that someone could do minimal work, and due to existing power structures, realize more benefit than someone with less power.

  2. Caricaturing or using art in a harmful way.

Can people enjoy banjo music without acknowledging its Appalachian Caribbean African Middle Eastern roots?

When we do creative acts, we’re making a sort of spiritual offering to the world. When I make a piece of pottery, it’s artistic impact is related to how much the piece resonates with others and gets them excited. For some pieces, they’re only meaningful to me, and thus I retain de facto “control” of the expression—no one else really cares about it. But for art that is more widely appreciated, there’s a component of the art that I don’t actually “own”. Art is the relationship between a creator expressing an idea, and culture receiving it.

An artist can always create art for self-expression, only. However, sharing art is sharing an idea, and ideas propagate mimetically. Many a musical artist has written a song that was received differently than originally intended — this is more of a feature than a bug.

Allocating social and economic value correctly

Protecting artistic intent

To what extent should we protect artistic intent? For example, if I sell a piece, which is then used to make fun of me, should I have recourse? Current copyright law falls squarely on “no”: when I sell the piece, I lose rights to how it is used.

What if I create something that is spiritually significant to me? When art is shared, I necessarily lose complete control — the same piece may be meaningful to someone else, but for entirely different reasons.

What if I created a form? For example, if I created a new genre of music with consistent sounds, grammar, etc, to what extent do I “own” the form? This case seems fairly straight forward to me: I should be able to reserve few protections. Every piece I create of this form is an artistic offering to culture, and the more it resonates with others, the more my form will inspire new artists to build upon it.

In fact, we have—and, should have—minimal protections around preserving intent of a piece. All art is derivative, and is a contribution to culture. Parodies, protected by fair use, abuse intent by design: they take art with a distinctive form, and transform it sufficiently to not compete economically, and reap all the social and economic upside.

What’s the difference, then, between culturally owned creations and personally owned creations? A similar approach can be taken: my culture “created” an artistic style or form, and collectively, we should receive the social and economic upside. If someone copies or adapts this artistic form, they’re taking recognition (social value) and money (economic value) that counterfactually could have been mine.

This is the case of Black ownership of TikTok dance trends. A culture, with unique history, heritage, and values, contributes something artistically to the world (in this case, dances). Another group, with more social and economic power, directly emulates the dances or style. If no one watched or appreciated the copies, no one would care. However, people did care: the derivative dances spread widely to much social and economic credit, leaving the original creators without recognition.

Let’s find the injustice. The counterfactual we’re seeking is one where Black dancers (a) retain control over their work, and/or (b) are appropriately recognized for their contribution. The former is difficult to justify, because the content was posted for distribution. In fact, the only way it was copied was because they had some distribution of their artistic ideas. So, the injustice has to be about social and economic upside that wasn’t captured, but should have been.

Interestingly, “culturally owned” art is maximally derivative: the more specific forms (Native American art, historical Japanese art) are cohesive around shared cultural values and identity, the less individual creators contributed innovation.

I need to tighten up my thinking about it.

First pass, I believe doing a creative act is offering something spiritual to the world. For example, when you paint a painting, it’s impact is basically how much it resonates with others and gets them excited about it. As such, you don’t really “own” the art. Art is always between the creator and culture — if an artist wanted to just do self-expression, they’d keep it private (which they can totally do).

We want to encourage both the creation, propagation, and remixing of good art.

Incentivizing creation (beyond intrinsic self-expression) is a function of recognition (i.e., social status) and compensation (which is sort of social status — the ability to convert creative expression into other useful goods and services). Both of these are probably good to embrace and systemically encourage. Someone re-using my art only takes away from recognition and compensation

Some creatives want to double dip: ownership of intent, as well as recieving the economic upside.

what I want to say:

  1. all art is derivative, and is in part a contribution to society.
  2. literally no work is made in isolation, so no one truly can claim they “own” an idea.
  3. obviously, it’s important to economically and socially reward creation, so we get more creation.
  4. there is a limit to the extent creatives own the impact of their work. each work serves as a template for future iteration.
  5. explicit goals: (prescriptive)
  6. Promote creative expression.
  7. Propagate good artistic ideas, encouraging iteration and re-use to create derivative works. Minimize rent seeking.
  8. Provide protection of how certain art is used. Cultural impact and sensitivity.
  9. when art is emulated, if it’s not economically substitutable, then there’s no real injustice.
  10. Africans copying Country Music doesn’t hurt Country Music. It’s not economically substitutable, and Country Music already has all the power. No one really cares.
  11. Americans copying Japanese aesthetic (“Cultural Appropriation”) in a vacuum doesn’t directly impact Japanese people, but has three potential harms: 1. Counterfactual economic loss. Potentially, Japanese people could have made money or had social recognition (power), but their art was appropriated and they were left out of any upside. 2. Harm of sacred values (“belittling”, “caricaturing”, “misusing”). This is less about economic or social impact and more about respect. If Chinese people made disposable napkins with Bible passages on it, it may feel harmful. Sacred ceremonies that are emulated by people not actually following the ceremony feels disrespectful. 3. Loss of control of heritage (“this is why we’re special”). Culture value comes from differences (recognizability), and the more it’s accepted, the fewer differences exist. Explicit recognition may decrease as ideas enter the general zeitgeist, but is equalled by cultural acceptance.

Idea: cultures should copy the best ideas from other cultures. That’s how progress gets made.

Side point: there is a difference between cultural enforced norms and legally enforced norms. Hot take: each art form will have different norms, and will self-police. Different art forms will have different cultural expectations around proper remixing etiquite. For example, dance does not retain much overship over specific dances, but a dance presented as “freestyle” that copies someone else is not regarded as cool. Rappers sample music, but do not do covers. Pop musicians do covers. Artists don’t typically own a style.

I think more controversially, I think the only concern for making sure economic and social credit is given is considering counterfactuals. As in, a reality where they would have been better off without the copying.

There’s a difference between legal protections (which primarily protect economic interests), and social protections (which primarily protect social interests).
